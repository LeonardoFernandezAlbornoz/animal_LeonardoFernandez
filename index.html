THE EMPEROR PENGUIN

Features and characteristics

  • Scientific name: Aptenodytes forsteri
  • Genus: Aptenodytes
  • Family: Spheniscidae
  • Order: Sphenisciformes
  • Class: Aves
  • Phylum: Chordata
  • Kingdom: Animalia

The emperor penguin, whose scientific name is Aptenodytes forsteri, belongs
to the family Spheniscidae and is classified as a species of sphenisciform bird, that is, it
is part of the flightless seabirds that inhabit only the southern hemisphere.

Description of the emperor penguin

  1. Height: Between 120 and 140 centimeters
  2. Weight: around 40kg
  3. Habitat: Antartica

This species can reach a height of up to 1.22 meters, which makes it the largest and
heaviest penguin, unlike the other penguins. Their weight varies from 22 to 45 kg, the
males being heavier.

The body of these birds, like other penguin species, is adapted to regulate their temperature to
environmental conditions and their hydrodynamic shape allows them to minimize water resistance
when in contact with water. Their rigid, flat wings are as strong as flippers, allowing them to
move easily through the Antarctic seas. They have 15 feathers per square centimeter, making them
the birds with the largest amount of plumage.

Feeding and predators

Basically the diet of the emperor penguin consists of fish, crustaceans such as krill and cephalopods
such as octopus and squid, but mainly the so-called Antarctic Devil (type of fish) occupies the largest
percentage in the diet of this species.

The tongue of the emperors contains barbs directed backwards so that their prey cannot escape easily.
They mostly search for prey in the open sea of the Antarctic Ocean or in crevices that form in the
ice shelves.

It is the only penguin species that can dive for up to eighteen minutes and to a depth of 500 meters. Thanks
to the hemoglobin generated by its organism, it is able to accomplish the arduous task of searching for food
at such depths. They also have solid bones to reduce the pressure between their body and the water, as well
as the ability to slow down their metabolism and pause organ functions that are not necessary at that moment.

Among their predators, which mainly hunt chicks, are the giant Antarctic petrel and the polar skua. Adult
penguins are preyed upon by killer whales and leopard seals
